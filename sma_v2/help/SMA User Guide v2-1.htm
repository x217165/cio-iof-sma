SMA – Service Management Administration

SMA is a software tool, created by TELUS, to help make the tasks of creating and managing Network Services more efficient. It is an interface to the ESD (Enhanced Services Database).

Customer orders are:

Created in SRT and DOCS

Tracked through ECOPS

Designed on Visio

Trouble reporting is via Remedy and T2

Billed on Arbor

SMA is a critical link in TELUS’s ability to provide ‘Service Management’ because it integrates all the activities and data that we need to manage to meet out contracts with our customers.

Detailed of processes for the Enhanced Services are found at

http://tacjagr1.office.tac.net/esdreports/esots/index.html

Change Control Procedures –User Group

 

    1. Role of the User Group

The User Group is made up of the Super Users identified in the SMA Process Ownership Model plus additional representatives from the IT department who represent systems that may be affected by the proposed changes.

The role of the Super User is to represent their peer group in the ownership of process, function and data for their area of responsibility. Super user responsibilities include:

  1. Identify and collect ideas for improvements from peer users.
  2. Analyze the impact of the improvement with regards to their function(s).
  3. Responsibly escalate improvement ideas and issues that overlap functional boundaries with the User Group for discussion.
  4. Participate in issue resolution to ensure the needs of the entire "Service Management" process are met.
  5. Update User Procedures document to reflect changes.
  6. Disseminate new and changed information to their peer group.

The role of the User Group Meeting is to serve as a forum where all issues regarding any process, data or system function can be shared with the other Super Users and IT representatives. The purpose is to fully understand the implications that a change or addition may have on other groups in the process. The primary responsibility of the User Group Meeting is to:

  1. Review and prioritize improvement ideas raised by Super Users since the previous meeting.
  2. Resolve issues that affect more than one functional area.
  3. Ensure system change requests are logged in the C3S program
  4. Review and approve change requests implemented in the latest release.
    1. Change Process

Each Super User is responsible for issue resolution and maintenance of documentation within their functional areas.

Any change relating to DATA, PROCESS or SYSTEM should follow the following process:

  1. Conduct a preliminary analysis of the change request.

    2. Ensure the change request is valid and that it cannot be solved with training. If the change request is valid, identify the groups that may be affected by the change. Get input from the identified Super Users regarding the potential impact of the change request.

  2. Log the change request as an item on the user group meeting agenda.

    3. E-mail a note to the User Group Coordinator (currently Lee Ettinger). The note should contain a short description of the issue and an estimate of the amount of time it will take to fully discuss the issue in the meeting. Try to be realistic in your time estimate so that the meeting can be planned to cover all items.

     

    AT THE USER GROUP MEETING

  3. Review and Discuss items on the agenda. The Super User responsible for logging the item should give background information on the change request for the rest of the user group.
  4. If it is determined by the user group that the change request is required and valuable continue with step 5, otherwise the issue should be closed and the Super User responsible for logging the item should communicate the resolution to the original requestor.
  5. If the change request results in enhancements or changes to the system, log the change request in the C3S application.
  6. If the change request results in process or data changes, each Super User is responsible to update the new process in the Procedures Document and also to communicate the new procedures to their peer user group.

      2. How Remedy Uses Contact Information
        1. Phone Contact

          2. There are often many contacts for each customer, and each contact can have one or multiple roles assigned to them. The important thing to remember is that these contacts, in the database are used by Remedy T2 and C2, and by Operations to provide contact information when alarms are detected on TAC equipment located at their sites. These automatic alarms create Remedy T2 tickets, which will have customer contact information attached.

          By default, Remedy will attach the SITE1 contact to any automatic (Spectrum created) trouble ticket on any specific element. This will only happen if a customer contact is defined as a SITE contact and the specific element is linked to that site location and customer in the SMA Managed Objects. Otherwise, if no site contact is available, the PRIM1 will be attached. If neither a SITE1 nor PRIM1 is defined for the customer, the contact information in the ticket will be blank. However, all contacts can be queried via the pull down menu on the ticket. The SERV1 contact is only used for E-Mail notification.

          It is valuable to have site contacts when the OSC requires confirmation of power and physical connectivity, as in most cases, the tickets are created by the systems and not called in by the customer. In cases where we have customers that want a single point of contact from a networking perspective, then SITE contacts should not be defined, and the PRIME1 should be the customer helpdesk or main technical contact (7x24).

        2. E-Mail Contact

Contact information is also used by Operations when sending email notification to customers for upcoming maintenance window activity that will affect their service, and for customer notifications and updates when a global outage occurs that affects multiple customers and services.

From both C2 and T2, an email distribution can be created for the customers that are linked to the service tags on any change ticket or trouble ticket. As a result of service correlation, any element that has a change or trouble ticket can have an email distribution list created for quick and easy notification. Obviously, this is only possible if the customer contacts are defined properly in the ESD and their correct email addresses are also entered.

Email notification (for all customer services) will be sent to the following customer roles:

  1. PRIM1 (Helpdesk- email should be the helpdesk email or an email that distributes to multiple contacts within the organization).
  2. PRIM2 (Manager or Lead Communication Analyst)
  3. SERV1 (Technical contact for a specific service i.e. Web Hosting).

The only time a SERV1 contact needs to be defined is if a specific customer has multiple services, and for some of these services they would like a different person contacted. We find this to be the case when a customer has purchased both Internetworking and Internet Hosting services. Often the customer’s organization is such that different people are responsible for the LAN/WAN and the Hosting Services.

        1. Change Control

          2. Another Role is the CHANGE role. This is used by operations to confirm that a specific customer contact has the authority to request changes to an existing service. The customer change process allows a customer to request a change by emailing the OSC with the details. This email must be received from an authorized change requestor for that customer. So, if a customer is going to be requesting changes, the contacts that are authorized must have CHANGE roles assigned in the ESD.

Customer contact information is defined and maintained by Customer Service Management. It is very important that they ensure that customer contacts are correct and have the proper roles assigned to them. Service Management should verify that the contact information is correct when they do their quarterly customer service reviews.

    2. Activating/Terminating the Customer Service

    There are 3 valid Status Codes for a customer service.

    DESIGN – Implementation Managers are responsible for adding new customer services. New customer services have a status of design. A design status indicates that the customer service is not being monitored or supported by the Operations Service Centre (OSC) nor is the customer being invoiced for the service.

    OPERATIONAL – The Operations Service Centre (OSC) is responsible for changing the status of a customer service to operational. An operational status indicates that the customer is free to use the service, OSC is fully monitoring and supporting the customer service and Customer Care is invoicing the customer for the service.

    TERMINATED – The Operations Service Centre (OSC) is responsible for changing the status of a customer service to terminated. A terminated status indicates that the customer can no longer access and use the service, OSC is no longer monitoring and supporting the customer service and Customer Care is no longer billing the customer for that service.

    To ACTIVATE a customer Service, set the Customer Service Status to ‘Operational’. The system will automatically set the Date In-Service to the current date. This date can be overwritten.

    To DEACTIVATE a customer service, first remove all network elements that are correlated to the service (see Attaching Network Elements to the Customer Service (Correlation Management) process). Next set the Customer Service Status to ‘Terminated’. The system will automatically set the Date Terminated to the current date. This date can be overwritten.

    IMPORTANT:

    When a customer service is activated or deactivated, the Customer Service Manager and the Billing Specialist must be informed. The system will generate an E-mail note. Verify the recipients of the note and confirm that there is a Customer Service Manager and a Billing Specialist, if not add them to the recipient list. PRESS THE SEND BUTTON ON THE E-MAIL…DO NOT CANCEL THE E-MAIL MESSAGE.
